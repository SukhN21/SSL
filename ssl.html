What is SSL

SSL stands for Secure Sockets Layer. It’s a security protocol designed to provide encrypted communication over a network, most commonly used for securing data exchanged between web browsers and servers. SSL encrypts the data transmitted between your browser and the website you’re visiting, making it difficult for anyone to intercept or read the information. SSL also ensures that the website you’re communicating with is the legitimate site you think it is. It uses digital certificates issued by trusted Certificate Authorities (CAs) to verify the identity of the website. SSL helps ensure that the data sent and received hasn’t been tampered with during transmission.

What is TLS

TLS, or Transport Layer Security, is the successor to SSL (Secure Sockets Layer). It's a cryptographic protocol designed to provide secure communication over a network. It also uses digital certificates to authenticate the identity of the server. TLS is more secure than SSL because it incorporates improvements and fixes vulnerabilities found in SSL. TLS provides mechanisms to ensure that the data has not been altered during transmission.

How can I tell that my webpage is secure?

A secure webpage is one that implements various measures to protect data, ensure privacy, and maintain integrity during transmission.

  • Encryption: The data transmitted between the user's browser and the web server is encrypted using TLS or SSL.
  • Authentication: The webpage uses a valid SSL/TLS certificate issued by a trusted Certificate Authority (CA).
  • Secure Communication: The webpage is accessible via HTTPS rather than HTTP.
  • Security Headers: The site uses security headers like Content Security Policy (CSP), Strict-Transport-Security (HSTS), and X-Content-Type-Options to protect against various types of web-based attacks.

What does it mean to be secure?

To be secure means to be protected against various threats, vulnerabilities, and risks. This concept applies across many contexts.

  • Confidentiality: Ensuring that information is accessible only to those authorized to view it.
  • Integrity: Ensuring that data is accurate and unaltered during transmission or storage.
  • Resilience: Ensuring systems can withstand and recover from security incidents or failures.
  • Authentication: Confirming that users or systems are who they claim to be. This is typically achieved through mechanisms like usernames and passwords, biometrics, or multi-factor authentication.

What is encryption and why do I care?

Encryption is a process that transforms readable data into an unreadable format using a cryptographic algorithm and a key. This transformation makes it difficult for unauthorized parties to access or understand the original data.

You should care about encryption because it plays a critical role in protecting your personal information and maintaining your privacy in the digital world. Here’s why encryption is important to you:

  • Sensitive Information: Encryption secures your personal details, such as your social security number, credit card information, and health records, from unauthorized access.
  • Mitigates Risks: In the event of a data breach, encrypted data is much harder for hackers to use. This means that even if your data is stolen, it remains encrypted and unusable without the proper decryption key.
  • Confidential Communication: Encryption keeps your emails, messages, and other communications private, ensuring that only the intended recipient can read them.

Do the prefixes HTTP or HTTPS matter when we're talking about secure websites?

Yes, the prefixes HTTP and HTTPS are crucial when discussing secure websites, and they indicate different levels of security for web communications.

What is an SSL certificate authority?

An SSL Certificate Authority (CA) is an organization that issues digital certificates used to secure communications over the internet.
